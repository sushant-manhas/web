🏡 Our prediction of 2026 housing market

The bulls capitulated. We didn't.

The NAR just slashed its 2026 existing-home sales forecast from +14% to +4% in April. A whopping ten-point cut.

That's the loudest signal the housing market has sent all year. And almost nobody is reading it correctly.

We predicted home prices would rise 4% in 2023 while everyone else was calling a crash. We were right. We're making another call now.

We've been tracking 24 forecasters, 300 metros, and the Freddie Mac weekly print since January.

Here it is.

Rates are stuck in the 6s.

The 30-year fixed mortgage rate hit 6.38% on March 26 — a six-month high, and the fourth straight weekly rise.

A month earlier, it briefly dipped below 6% for the first time since 2022.

Then the Iran war escalated. Oil spiked. Markets priced the Fed back to "higher for longer."

The 21-firm consensus pegged the 2026 average at 6.18%. We're already above it.

Morgan Stanley's 5.75% call looks ambitious. Hunter's 6.6% looks about right.

The spread between the 10-year Treasury and the 30-year mortgage is 207 basis points. The historical average is 176. The bond market does not believe the Fed.

Meanwhile, January existing-home sales fell 8.4% month-over-month — the biggest drop since February 2022. March printed a nine-month low at 3.98 million SAAR.

So, the NAR cut.

The lock-in cage is the market.

7 out of 10 outstanding mortgages are locked below 5%.

Typical homeowner tenure is now 12 years — nearly double 20 years ago. Boomers own 28% of all 3+ bedroom homes. They aren't selling.

This is why supply cannot normalize. This is why prices grind higher even with 6.38% rates and the weakest builder sentiment in seven months.

In December, there were 600,000 more sellers than buyers across the U.S. — the highest on record in seasonally adjusted data going back to 2013.

You'd think 600,000 excess sellers would crash prices, right?

Wrong!

Prices still rose. The March median existing-home price hit $408,800, up 1.4% year-over-year.

Why?

Because the "sellers" aren't really selling. They're listing, watching, and pulling back. 62% of 2025 buyers paid below listing — the highest share since 2019 — and the median price still climbed.

The lock-in beats the listing gap. Every time.

There is no "national" housing market. There are two.

99 of the 300 largest U.S. metros — one in three — posted year-over-year price declines in February 2026.

Hartford, Connecticut sits 21.2% above its 2022 peak. Austin, Texas sits 27.9% below.

Same country. Same rates. Opposite trajectories.

Zillow's Home Value Index for March tells the same story across the top 20 metros. Chicago +4.2%, New York +4.1%, Detroit +3.0%, Philadelphia +2.7%, Minneapolis +1.9%, Boston +1.6%. Tampa −4.3%, Miami −3.9%, Dallas −3.7%, Denver −3.1%, Atlanta −2.5%, Houston −2.1%, Phoenix −2.0%.

The Northeast and Midwest are tight. The Sun Belt and Mountain West are correcting into year four.

Zillow's own 1-year forecast: Seattle −2.1%, Denver −3.0%, San Francisco −2.9%. These markets are not done falling.

Our prediction

The 24-forecaster consensus says U.S. home prices rise 1.43% in 2026. We agree with the direction. We disagree with what the number means.

National home prices will rise 1–2% in 2026 — and by year-end that average will hide a six-to-eight-point gap between the best and worst major metros.

Three things are going to happen.

First, the Midwest and Northeast keep grinding higher. Chicago, New York, Philadelphia, Detroit, and Boston end the year up 3–5%.

Affordability dollars are rotating east and north. Redfin's own 2026 "hot markets" list already named the NYC suburbs and the Great Lakes.

Second, the Sun Belt correction bottoms, not recovers. Tampa, Miami, Dallas, Denver, and Austin print another negative year — but the declines will shrink. The correction that started in 2022 is closer to the end than the beginning.

Third, sales volume stays stuck near 4 million. The NAR's revised +4% forecast is still optimistic.

Lock-in does not release at 6.38%. It might release at 5.5%. We don't see 5.5% this year.

What could prove us wrong

The Iran war escalates and rates cross 7%. Oil holds above $100. The Fed freezes. Sales collapse below 3.8 million SAAR. Prices go flat-to-negative nationally by Q4.

The Fed cuts aggressively and rates fall below 5.5%. Lock-in releases. Inventory floods the market. Prices still rise — but 3–4%, not 1–2% — and the Sun Belt recovers faster than we predict.

Builders flood the Sun Belt with rate buy-downs. 2/3 of builders offered incentives in February, and big builders are advertising buy-down rates at 5% or under. If they keep clearing inventory at sub-5% advertised rates, new-home supply pressures Sun Belt resale prices harder than our "correction bottoms" call allows.

Florida turns faster than we predict. PulteGroup reports demand picking up in Florida; Zillow's ZHVI still has Miami at −3.9% and Tampa at −4.3%. If PulteGroup's on-the-ground read is early, Sun Belt stabilization is a mid-2026 story, not a late one.

Job losses cascade. February payrolls already fell 92,000. If that's the start of a trend and not a blip, the median household can't afford the median home regardless of rates.

We're watching three things: the Freddie Mac weekly rate print, the NAHB monthly builder survey, and the non-farm payrolls report. If two of the three turn, our call turns with them.

Everything else is noise.

Expect home prices to keep rising in 2026 — in the cities nobody is watching.
